Governance_&_Reporting.md

Moon Ice Crawler — Phase 3

1. Purpose of This Document

This document defines how governance remains real and visible over time, and how reporting ensures honesty instead of politics.

Governance isn’t a slide deck.
Governance is behavior you can see, audit, and trust.

Reporting is not PR.
Reporting is structured truth delivery.

2. Governance Philosophy

Three execution truths apply:

1️⃣ Authority exists only if it can be seen working
2️⃣ Reporting matters only if it cannot be gamed
3️⃣ Transparency is a safety function, not a courtesy

If the crawler succeeds “quietly but with hidden risk,” the program failed governance.

3. Governance Responsibilities
3.1 Strategic Authority

Responsible for:

long-horizon accountability

risk posture guardianship

doctrine integrity defense

rejecting politicized pressure

accepting accountability for major decisions

Strategic Authority does not:

manage tactical execution

bypass safety / oversight

suppress reality for optics

3.2 Operations Command

Responsible for:

day-to-day execution discipline

adherence to doctrine

honest reporting upward

operator safety and support

Operations Command does not:

minimize incidents to look good

override governance without process

hide shortfalls or inconvenient truths

3.3 Engineering Oversight

Responsible for:

technical truth grounding

reliability tracking

anomaly analysis

risk interpretation

Engineering does not:

force operations to gamble

sugar-coat uncertainty

allow optimism to override evidence

3.4 Independent Oversight

Responsible for:

auditing governance behavior

validating doctrine compliance

drift detection

whistleblower protection

ability to halt if governance collapses

Oversight must have teeth.
If it doesn’t, it’s theater.

4. Reporting Architecture

Reporting follows structured layers so truth cannot be filtered away.

4.1 Operator-Level Reporting

Produced after every operational cycle.

Must include:

execution summary

anomalies observed

checklist compliance status

safety incidents

deviations and explanations

operator confidence statement (mandatory subjective truth)

Subjective confidence matters.
If operators “feel uneasy,” leadership listens.

4.2 Operations Command Reports

Aggregated and periodic.

Include:

performance cadence vs expectation

stability assessment

abort history and rationale

risk trend identification

doctrine adherence assessment

recommendations

unresolved concerns

No KPI gamification allowed.
Numbers must illuminate reality, not sell it.

4.3 Engineering Reports

Include:

reliability analysis

subsystem health summaries

degradation tracking

comms reliability assessment

energy performance review

anomaly deep-dive reports

risk forecasts

If engineering cannot quantify risk,
leadership must assume higher risk until clarity achieved.

4.4 Oversight Reports

Include:

governance compliance confirmation

doctrine enforcement verification

drift detection findings

transparency health

trustworthiness assessment of reporting chain

If oversight cannot honestly say governance is healthy,
then governance is not healthy.

4.5 Strategic Authority Reports

Infrequent but crucial.

Include:

big-picture mission value assessment

long-term posture

accountability acknowledgement

continuation / modification / retirement recommendations

This ensures leadership cannot hide behind bureaucracy.

5. Reporting Cadence

Cadence may vary by mission needs, but expected baseline:

Operator cycle reports — every operational run

Ops Command report — weekly or equivalent operational period

Engineering reliability report — monthly

Oversight compliance report — quarterly

Strategic review — annually or at major milestone

Cadence adjustments require governance acknowledgement.

Silence is drift.
Silence is risk.

6. Transparency Rules

Transparency must be:

documented

accessible to required stakeholders

auditable

preserved long-term

Forbidden:

selective reporting

summary-only leadership views

report suppression

“do not write that down” culture

If leadership needs information hidden to feel comfortable,
leadership is the risk.

7. Incident Reporting

Incidents require special treatment:

1️⃣ immediate safety stabilization
2️⃣ incident declared
3️⃣ documented fact capture
4️⃣ escalation according to doctrine
5️⃣ independent review engaged
6️⃣ transparent reporting pathway enforced

Incidents must never be:

negotiated

minimized

politically suppressed

Incident honesty protects missions.

8. Drift and Governance Degradation Detection

Reporting must explicitly watch for:

shrinking reports

declining detail

fewer anomalies “reported” without cause

fewer aborts despite same risk environment

leadership pressure indicators

“everything is always perfect” trend

If everything suddenly looks perfect,
governance is likely failing.

9. Governance Failure Triggers

Governance is considered failed if:

oversight loses teeth

operators fear truth

reporting becomes self-censored

data integrity questionable

leadership avoids accountability

When governance fails:

program posture downgrades

operations slow or halt

corrective governance restructuring enforced

Execution without real governance is reckless.

10. Governance & Reporting Success Criteria

Governance & reporting succeed when:

truth flows upward consistently

leadership cannot hide from reality

oversight trusts data

operators trust leadership

decision-makers feel grounded, not sold

program performance reflects reality, not optimism

If people in the program feel safer providing partial truth than full truth,
Phase-3 governance has failed.
